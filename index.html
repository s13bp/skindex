How to Determine CSGO (CS2) Skins Value?

The value of your skins is a key factor in making successful trades, but determining it is not always so easy. In fact, this is where trading sites come in handy, provided that they display detailed information about skins. If you want to sell or trade CS:GO (CS2) skins, you need to know their condition, price range, float value, and pretty much everything there is to know about them. Being aware of these things will help you determine the value of each skin and decide whether your items are worth trading or have the potential for later profits. The Steam market is full of intricacies, which makes it that much more interesting. For example, did you know that one of the most expensive CSGO skins is AWP Dragon Lore, which sold for a record $61,000+?

StatTrak

This is a simple, but very important feature, especially when it comes to trading CS:GO (CS2) items. StatTrak skins are much more valuable than regular ones, and thus their prices are likely to be much higher. StatTrak is a feature that tracks the number of kills a player has achieved with a given skin. This makes the weapon much more interesting and unique. Listing StatTrak skins for trade on reputable sites such as SkinsMonkey is a great way to bring you a substantial profit.

Wear Levels

One of the most important determinants of the value of a particular CS:GO (CS2) skin is its Condition or Wear Level. There are 5 different levels of wear in Counter Strike, and each is accompanied by a specific range of float values. Here is a list of all of them from best to worst:

  • Factory New (FN)

  • Minimal Wear (MW)

  • Field-Tested (FT)

  • Well-Worn (WW)

  • Battle-Scarred (BS)

These wear levels usually determine the price range of a particular skin. As a rule of thumb, Factory New skins will be more expensive than Battle-Scarred ones, but this does not always apply. There are some exceptions to this rule, in which a certain Field-Tested skin may be worth more than a Factory New one. Sometimes even Battle-Scarred skins are slightly more expensive than Field-Tested, but such instances are very rare.

Rarity

Another important factor, which can sometimes be even more crucial than a skin's wear level, is its Rarity, which can also be referred to as Drop Probability. All CS:GO (CS2) skins are assigned a certain grade of rarity, which cannot be changed. In Counter-Strike, there are 7 different Grades. It doesn't matter if the skins you're considering are for the M4A1, Moto Gloves, Sport Gloves, Talon Knife, or anything in between, they all have Rarity Grades that add or subtract value to them. Here's a list of classes from most to least common:

  • Consumer Grade (white/gray) - Common

  • Industrial Grade (light blue) - Uncommon

  • Mil-spec (dark blue) - Rare

  • Restricted (purple) - Remarkable

  • Classified (hot pink) - Legendary

  • Covert (red) - Ancient

  • Exceedingly Rare (gold) - Rare Special, Gloves, Knives

  • Contraband Grade (brown) - Immortal

Ideally, the site you choose to trade CS items will display rarity grades, wear levels and other relevant information about the skins on offer. These are key factors that determine the market value of skins, and you can use them to your advantage. Thanks to them, you will know whether the trade you want to make will be profitable or not. If the market price of an item is great, then you can trade it for skins that may later sell better than yours. Read this article to learn how to check the value of your Counter Strike inventory.

Can You Get Scammed Trading CS:GO (CS2) Skins?

Scammers have always been and will always be present, no matter what industry or game you are in. Even the game industry is plagued by people who try to cheat other users by tricking them into handing over their valuable skins. Then, they can rip them off, profiting from such exchanges and sending nothing in return.

However, Valve Corporation, like other valuable platforms, is constantly updating its security features so that members of the Counter Strike community can enjoy safe, risk-free transactions and trading. Still, being cautious is key, as there are various ways you can be scammed online. Of course, this doesn't mean you have to monitor your Steam inventory 24/7, it just means you should keep an eye out for any suspicious activities and users. Perhaps offers you think are too good to be true really are too good to be true. Here are some examples of the most common scams you need to watch out for when trading skins:

Phishing Scam

This is one of the most common types of scams that can be found on the Internet. In fact, a Phishing Scam is a pretty clever way to trick someone. Typically, it is used by people who want to get someone to give out their personal information, such as contact information, username, and possibly passwords, without them even knowing it. It involves someone sending you a link leading to a copy of a trusted website, hoping that you will log in there using your real password and username. The way they lure you is by offering you extra good deals or free items. Once you go to that site and log in using your real password, you're pretty much done. If they have your info, they can easily take over your Steam Account.

The key is to not trust any shady users. If they're offering you something for free, they are probably trying to clear your balance and take away all of your most valuable skins. Also, always make sure the site you're using is secure and verified before you proceed with any transaction. And if you're eager to obtain some free skins, take a look at our Freebies - Free CS:GO Skins tab, where you will find several opportunities to get extra items! Don't fall for any phishing sites!

Showcase Scam

The Showcase Scam is less common and more difficult to execute than the Phishing Scam, but still possible. It works by gaining players' trust. Users will reach out to you, claiming to be popular streamers or YouTubers. Eventually, they will ask for some of your valuable skins to showcase on their channels. Once the items are sent, you'll probably never see them again.

Jackpot Scam

Another popular way to scam people out of money is the Jackpot Scam. This one, however, takes some planning and involves building a rather complicated website. The way it works is that someone will write you a message claiming to run a popular jackpot site (jackpot sites are basically CS gambling platforms). It will prompt you to send valuable skins with the promise that you will be chosen as the jackpot winner. These sites often use bots to run their scams to make themselves appear legitimate. Stay away from any such offers if you spot them.

CS Trading Language

Knowing the right language is another key feature that will improve your trading skills. You can't afford to make mistakes just because you didn't know what things meant. That being said, here are some key terms that most Counter Strike players need to know.

  • PS or Play Side - The side of the skin that can be seen while playing

  • BS or Back Side - The other side of the skin, which can be seen while inspecting the item

  • SS - Screenshot

  • C/O or Current/Offer - Best offer of a specific person

  • B/O or Buy/Out - a 100% accepted trade

  • Blue Gem - A skin that is at least 80% blue

  • Autobump - Automatic bump on CSGO Lounge Trades

  • Full Fade Knives - 100% of the knife is covered in a fade

  • K/k - CS GO Keys

  • Overpay - Someone who has to pay more than the value to get a specific item

  • Trade-Up - Trading Steam items and skins for a profit

  • Lowball - The action of offering a lot lower price than the seller expects

  • QS - Quick Selling - If someone needs a specific item they are likely to accept any offer which has the potential to generate quick profit

If you always keep up to date with what's going on the Steam Market, and make sure you are well-versed in the language used by other traders, your skin exchanges are bound to be profitable.
